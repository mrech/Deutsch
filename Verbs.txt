==================================================
HABEN: avere
==================================================
The verb haben (to have) is a very frequent irregular German verb. 
It indicates the possession of an object or a state referring to a noun. 
It is also used with fixed phrases. 
The object following the verb haben is in the ACCUSATIVE CASE.

          | PRÄSENS   
----------| ---------- 
Ich       | hab-E
Du        | ha-ST
Er/Sie/Es | ha-T           
Wir       | hab-EN          
Ihr       | hab-T         
Sie       | hab-EN           

E.G.: 
Sie hat kein Geld: Lei non ha soldi
Ich habe keinen platz in meiner Wohnung.: I have no space in my appartment
Ich habe ein Auto: I have a car
Jeremy hat eine Brille: Jeremy has glasses
Wir haben keine Zeit: We have no time
Ich habe dich lieb: I love you.

##################################################
################# SEPARABLE VERB #################
##################################################

These verbs are separated from the prefix in their conjugated form.
The respective prefix of a verb determines whether the verb is 
separated or not. The verb is always conjugated the same way as 
the simple verb without prefix.

E.G.:
--------------------------------------------------
aufwachen (to wake up): 
--------------------------------------------------
Ich wache auf. (I wake up.)

--------------------------------------------------
losfahren (to set off): 
--------------------------------------------------
Das Auto fährt los. (The car begins to drive.)

--------------------------------------------------
einkaufen (to buy): 
--------------------------------------------------
Mein Vater kauft Tomaten ein.(My father buys tomatoes.)


The most important prefixes that designate separable verbs: 
ab-, an-, auf-, aus-, ein-, gegen-, hoch-, los-, mit-, nach-, 
vor-, weg-, weiter-, zurück-, da-, hin-, her-, raus-, rein-, 
rüber- and runter-.

A separable verb separates from its prefix in the following scenarios:

> when conjugated: Er macht die Tür auf. (He opens the door.)
> when the participle II is formed, a -ge- is placed between 
the prefix and the root: 
Meine Tante ist mitgekommen. (My aunt came with us.)


A separable verb does not separate from its prefix 
if it appears together with a modal verb:

Wir müssen die Polizei anrufen.: We have to call the police.


The following prefixes never separate from their verb stem: 
be-, emp-, ent-, er-, ge-, ver- and zer-

##################################################
################# PAST PARTICIPLE ################
##################################################
The past participle is a verb form and used to build compound tenses
such as the present perfect, the past perfect, the passive voices
and the future perfect.


==================================================
REGULAR VERBS
==================================================
With regular verbs, the past participle is formed with the prefix GE-,
the root of the verb, and a -T at the end.

E.G.:
machen > gemacht: to make|to do > made|done
kochen > ge kocht: to cook > cooked 


When the root of the verb ends in -T or -D, an additional E is added. 
This happens also after some consonants to make it easier to pronounce.

E.G.:
/arbaiten/ arbeiten > gearbeitet: to work > worked
öffnen > geöffnet: to open > opened


Verbs that end with -IEREN or begin with inseparable prefixes 
such as BE-, ENT-, ER-, VER-, ZER- form the past participle 
without GE-.

E.G.:
telefonieren > telefoniert: to call > called
studieren > studiert: to study > studied
besuchen > besucht: to visit > visited
erzählen > erzählt: to tell > told


##################################################
################### MODAL VERB ###################
##################################################

Modal verbs appear together with a second verb most of the time to modify 
the meaning of the sentence. 
There are five modal verbs: 

> können (to be able to/to can)
> wollen (to want to)
> sollen (to be supposed to/should)
> müssen (to have to)
> dürfen (to be allowed to). 

The verb **mögen/möchten (to like to)** is a special kind of modal verb. 
It is used in the subjunctive (see below: Subjunctive II).

In a sentence, the modal verb is conjugated and the second 
verb remains in the infinitive. The conjugated modal verb takes the 
second position in the sentence, while the full verb is at the end of the sentence.

##################################################
SUBJUNCTIVE II
##################################################
The subjective mood is used in hypothetical, potential, 
or uncertain situations. It is also used to express wishes 
and polite requests.

Present and Past Time
_____________________
In German, the subjunctive forms (subjunctive I and subjunctive II) 
can be expressed in either present or past time. 
Present time can also refer to an event that could happen in the future.

Conjugations
_____________________
Modal verbs in subjunctive II are formed like the simple past tense. 
However, modals that had an umlaut in the infinitive regain their 
umlaut in subjunctive II.

E.G.:
Ich möchte noch etwas Kaffee trinken.: 
I would like to drink some more coffee.
